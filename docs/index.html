Man-Made Disasters

Triangulated data methods: satellite imagery, surveys & socioeconomic outcomes
Elisabetta Pietrostefani · Univ. of Liverpool SPACERAISE 2026 · Week 3 · GSSI, L'Aquila · 19 May 2026

About this training

This training is part of the SPACERAISE 2026 Doctoral School module on Geospatial Data and Quantitative Analysis in Social Science at the Gran Sasso Science Institute (GSSI), L’Aquila, Italy.

It covers how to combine satellite remote sensing data with traditional survey data to measure the impacts of man-made disasters — illustrated with the case of the 2020 Beirut Port Explosion.

What you will learn

By the end of this training you will be able to:

  • Understand how satellite damage proxy maps are constructed from pre/post imagery
  • Spatially join and compare remote sensing and ground-truth survey data
  • Run and interpret regression models linking physical destruction to socioeconomic outcomes
  • Critically assess where different data sources agree — and where they diverge

Case study: Beirut Port Explosion (August 4, 2020)

The explosion at the Port of Beirut was one of the largest non-nuclear explosions in history, equivalent to approximately 1–1.5 kilotons of TNT. It devastated large parts of the city and provides a compelling case study for how we can use multiple data types — satellite imagery, household surveys, and socioeconomic indicators — to understand the spatial patterns of destruction and recovery.

NoteData note

This lab uses MAXAR satellite-derived damage data and a synthetic survey dataset modelled on a real in-person survey conducted in Beirut post-blast. The synthetic data preserves the statistical structure of the original while protecting respondent confidentiality.
